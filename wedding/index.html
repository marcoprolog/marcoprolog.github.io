How we met

It was the 5 th of October 2012. At the time both of us were attending the IT-university in Copenhagen in our respective programs without any particular chance of having an encounter. That weekend we each decided to participate in a gamejam, an event during which teams make video games together. That weekend our lives crossed as we by whim of fate were placed in the same team. We bonded instantly over tea, video games and laughing, all reoccurring items in our future lives together. Thinking back on that weekend, and the months to come, made sense of the previously overly romanticized concept of “soulmates”. Never have I befriended a person faster, and never before has it felt so right.

Somehow already seven years and some months have passed since then. A seemingly simultaneously meaningless and infinitely meaningful number, as after just one year it felt as if we’d known each other for ten and so we stopped counting before we’d even begun. Afterall, every moment spent with you is well spent.

Engagement

I proposed to Marco the 1st of July 2018. I had been planning it for likely around five months, and it took weeks to finalise the preparations. I wanted all elements to be special, intimate and meaningful to our shared history and to Marco as a person. The rings I had custom made to be identical except sizes, silver and a symbolic red ruby for many reasons, mostly due to red being Marco’s favourite colour, and I thought it would suit his hair. The ring box I made from scratch to appear as a miniature and exact copy of the treasure chests in one of Marco’s favourite video games of all time: The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time. This was done by having my dear friend and maid of honour Fatima 3D-print it, then I painted it like wood and installed a light-triggered sound board to play the ‘chest-opening-sound effect from the game.

The location was the main wild card. That summer we would go to Japan, and it seemed adventurous and romantic, but it was deprived of a shared meaning and history. Prior we would visit Italy. Possible lacking subtly due to excitement and urgency, I suggested we visited Duno. The Saturday after our arrival Marco and I woke early and alone hiked to the top, which we’d only done once prior the first year we visited Italy together back in 2013. At the top, with a breath-taking view of the valley below, I suggested we took a picture. I claimed setting a timer, when in fact I started filming, as I placed Marco’s phone angled against a nearby bench. I then fell to my knee, revealed my secret bounty and popped the question. “Yes!”

- Anita

Speeches and games during dinner (Festlige indslag)

During Danish weddings (and other major celebratory events) it is a tradition to have so-called “festlige indslag”, which can be understood to be moments and breaks of entertainment planned by guests during the party dinner. These can be anything as the limitation is the imagination and ingenuity of the guests. Traditionally these are mainly speeches or small participatory games, where either the groom and bride are the sole participates to the entertainment of the guests (and newlyweds) or all guests are participants in games usually centred around the newlyweds. We more than welcome speeches, games and more during the dinner, but to avoid cold food and agitated kitchen personal we ask you to report and coordinate all such “festlige indslag” with our diligent toastmaster Dennis before the party, so he can make a “plan of attack” with the venue.
Additionally, some of our Danish friends and family can be contacted if you have questions in regard to this fairly open-to-interpretation Danish tradition, or better need help pulling off more elaborate elements of entertainment.
Especially in regard to speeches the language barrier can become a problem. We do not have a finite all-round solution, but if you want to do anything that isn’t in English, then having your speech or other translated prior to the wedding into English/Danish/Italian is something that we can hope to coordinate.
